# Ember Design Principles: Foundational "Guiding Lights" for a Dense, Investment-First FIRE Planning App

## TL;DR

- **Adopt eight cross-cutting design laws** grounded in visual-cognition research (Cleveland–McGill, Gestalt, cognitive load theory, Shneiderman's mantra, Norman's principles, information foraging) and validated against the best comparable tools (ProjectionLab, Boldin, Kubera, Monarch, Copilot): a unified table model, a strict two-channel feedback convention (toast vs. banner), a transparent assumptions system as a first-class IA citizen, a persistent global scenario indicator, a tightened 3+2 button taxonomy, redundant (never color-alone) semantic encodings, progressive disclosure for dense data, and standardized empty/loading/error states.
- **The single most consequential recommendation:** make assumptions a dedicated, top-level destination separated from profile/household settings, with a persistent global "active scenario" indicator in the top nav — this directly mirrors what Boldin had to retrofit (its November 26, 2024 top-nav scenario-toggle relocation) and what ProjectionLab does well, and it operationalizes Ember's "nothing hidden" differentiator into measurable trust (Fogg's prominence-interpretation theory).
- **Keep the 7-section nav but regroup it conceptually:** the investment-first split (Accounts, Holdings, Activity = "the ledger"; Flows, Budget, Planning = "the model"; Settings) is defensible, but Assumptions should be surfaced as its own wayfinding anchor rather than buried in Settings or Planning, and breadcrumbs/scenario context must be added to close documented wayfinding gaps.

## Key Findings

1. **Position and length beat color and area for quantitative judgment.** Cleveland & McGill's empirically ranked hierarchy (position along a common scale > length/direction/angle > area > volume/curvature > shading/color saturation) means Ember's projection and net-worth charts should encode the most important quantitative comparisons with aligned bars/lines, and treat color as a categorical/redundant channel, not a primary quantitative one. The authors themselves framed it as "a framework within which to work," not a rigid prescription.
2. **Working memory is the binding constraint; extraneous load is the enemy.** Cognitive Load Theory (Sweller) and chunking (Miller) argue that the path to a legible dense UI is ruthless elimination of extraneous load (inconsistent tables, competing feedback systems, button sprawl) so the user's limited capacity is spent on the intrinsic complexity of their finances.
3. **Consistency is a force multiplier (Jakob's Law + Norman).** Every page that reimplements a table, an "add" button, or a delete confirmation differently forces users to rebuild a mental model, violating Norman's mapping/consistency principles and Nielsen's "consistency and standards" heuristic.
4. **Trust is designed, and transparency is its mechanism.** Fogg's prominence-interpretation theory and web-credibility research show credibility is inferred from prominent, interpretable cues. Ember's auditable assumptions (date stamps, source badges, append-only history) are not a feature bolt-on — they are the primary trust-production engine and should be among the most prominent, discoverable elements.
5. **Best-in-class tools converge on the same patterns.** ProjectionLab is loved for transparent, tweakable assumptions, named scenario comparison ("Compare" mode with baseline as a dashed line), and a centralized "Plan Settings" hierarchy. Boldin had to retrofit a top-nav scenario indicator after discoverability complaints. Kubera is praised for a clean, spreadsheet-like dense table and "everything in one place," but criticized for shallow planning. These are direct, evidence-backed templates for Ember.
6. **Color alone fails about 1 in 12 men.** Per Colour Blind Awareness, "Colour (color) blindness… affects approximately 1 in 12 men (8%) and 1 in 200 women in the world" (consistent with MedlinePlus Genetics for Northern European ancestry). WCAG 1.4.1 (Level A) therefore requires that green/red gain/loss always be paired with a sign, icon, or position — a hard rule, not a preference.
7. **"Overview first, zoom and filter, details on demand"** (Shneiderman) is the organizing principle for dense expandable financial tables: show summary rows, let users filter, and reveal tax lots/holdings on demand rather than all at once.
8. **Toast vs. banner is a solved problem** with a clear convention: transient toasts for non-critical action confirmations; persistent inline banners/alerts for state that must not be missed or that requires action. Two competing systems used interchangeably is the anti-pattern.

## Details

### Principle 1 — One table system, three density modes (unify the tables)

**Stated crisply:** Ember should have a _single_ data-table component with configurable density modes (compact, dense+expandable, wide+sparse), not per-page reimplementations.

**Evidence.** Gestalt principles of proximity, similarity, and common region govern how users perceive rows, columns, and groupings; alignment and uniform spacing are what let the eye scan a column as a unit. The "tabular/lining numerals" convention is foundational: tabular figures share an identical width so digits "line up neatly in columns," which Datawrapper notes makes it "easy to see instantly how many figures a number has, because a 124.17 will be the same length as a 680.90." This is precisely why Ember's choice of Roboto Mono for numerals is correct — but it must be enforced _system-wide via a shared component_, not reimplemented. Cognitive Load Theory says inconsistent table implementations inject pure extraneous load. Shneiderman's mantra dictates the interaction model: summary row → filter → expand for tax lots/holdings (details on demand). Kubera's reviewers consistently praise its "spreadsheet-like" interface that is "intuitive" and shows data "without overwhelming you with unnecessary charts," validating the dense-but-legible target.

**Implication for Ember.** Build one `<DataTable>` with: right-aligned, tabular-figure numeric columns; a standard expand affordance (chevron/disclosure) for row-level details-on-demand; consistent zebra/grouping using common-region borders sparingly; column-priority metadata baked in (see Principle 8/mobile). Compact mode for summaries, dense+expandable for holdings→tax lots, wide+sparse for activity/transactions. Right-align numbers, left-align text labels — the alignment itself is the scanning aid.

### Principle 2 — A strict two-channel feedback convention (resolve toast vs. alert)

**Stated crisply:** Transient **toasts** confirm completed, non-critical actions and auto-dismiss; persistent **inline banners/alerts** carry state that must not be missed, errors, or anything requiring action, and persist until resolved or dismissed.

**Evidence.** This is the documented industry consensus. The IBM Carbon Design System notification pattern: toasts "are more disruptive than inline notifications and are best used with system-generated messages that do not correspond to a specific section of the UI," and "Don't use notifications that dismiss on a timer for critical or emergency messages… timed actionable toasts may not provide sufficient time" (citing WCAG 2.1 success criterion 2.2.4). UX Movement: a banner alert is for "something important related to the system but don't need them to act immediately… Don't use this alert in response to an individual user's actions." PatternFly: inline alerts for "form validation and warning messages," placed in the page header if global or inline if specific. Nielsen's "visibility of system status" heuristic requires _some_ feedback for every action; the failure mode is using two systems interchangeably, which is extraneous load.

**Implication for Ember.** Pick the convention: (a) **Toast** = ephemeral success/confirmation ("Holding added," "CSV imported"), top-right, auto-dismiss ~4–8s, max one to three stacked, never carrying critical info; (b) **Inline banner/alert** = validation errors, sync failures, "this scenario is stale," anything needing a decision — placed contextually (page header for global, inline for field-specific), persistent, manually dismissible. Errors _never_ go in a timed toast. Document this as a single rule in the design system and delete the redundant pathway.

### Principle 3 — Assumptions are a first-class IA citizen, separated from profile/household settings

**Stated crisply:** Planning assumptions (return rates, inflation, life expectancy, tax assumptions) belong in a dedicated, top-level "Assumptions" destination — _not_ commingled with profile/household settings, and not buried in a generic Settings bucket.

**Evidence — the strongest competitive signal in this report.** Boldin (formerly NewRetirement) **deliberately separated assumptions from profile data**. Per Boldin's release notes: "On June 24, 2025 we removed the Profile and Goals section of My Plan. You may now access your birth date and name, as well as add and remove a spouse in Member Settings. Your retirement and longevity ages can be found in the My Plan Summary." Planning assumptions live in dedicated **My Plan > Rate Assumptions** and **My Plan > Assumptions > Taxes** areas. ProjectionLab consolidates assumptions in a hierarchical **"Plan Settings"** with clean plan-level → account-level override semantics (an account can "Follow your plan's Investment and Dividend Yield assumptions" or "Apply a Fixed Rate specific to this account"). Information-foraging theory (Pirolli & Card) explains _why_ this matters: users follow "information scent" — navigation labels that predict where a target lives. An "Assumptions" label has strong scent for "where do I change my return rate?"; burying it under "Settings" or "Profile" weakens the scent and raises foraging cost. Fogg's prominence-interpretation theory adds the trust dimension: assumptions that are prominent and interpretable produce credibility; hidden ones cannot.

**Implication for Ember.** Promote **Assumptions** to a first-class, labeled destination (either its own top-level entry or a prominent, clearly-scented anchor within Planning), distinct from **Settings** (which holds account/household/profile/preferences). Within Assumptions, implement plan-level defaults with per-account/per-line overrides (the ProjectionLab model). The date-stamps, source badges, and append-only history should live here and be visually prominent — this is Ember's differentiator and its trust engine. A concrete model for source-badged transparency: Boldin publicly documents its default long-term-care assumption against a named source — "According to the most recent Genworth survey, the median cost of care in an assisted living facility in the United States in 2024 was $5,900 per month," which it bakes into default modeling. Note, however, that Boldin's split is _functional but scattered_ (assumptions live across Rate Assumptions, Assumptions>Taxes, and Summary), which reviewers found contributes to a steep learning curve; Ember should consolidate rather than scatter, following ProjectionLab's single hierarchy.

### Principle 4 — A persistent, global "active scenario" indicator (close the silent-state wayfinding gap)

**Stated crisply:** Whenever a scenario/what-if selection silently changes results across multiple pages, a _persistent, global_ indicator must show which scenario is active, at all times, in the top-level chrome.

**Evidence — a documented retrofit by a competitor.** Boldin relocated its scenario control specifically to fix this. Per its release notes: "On November 26, 2024 we moved the Scenario Toggle and updated the top navigation bar to improve scenario visibility and ease of use," and it added a "My Plan Summary page that enables you to see a snapshot of the scenario you are in." The very existence of this update is evidence that a non-persistent scenario indicator is a real, shipped-and-then-fixed UX failure. Boldin also documents a related confusion: account balances are _shared_ across scenarios, and a Bogleheads user called this "super confusing," suggesting Boldin "implement a brief explanation notification or warning." ProjectionLab's "Compare" mode signals scenario state visually — the baseline plan renders as a _dashed line_ with the modified plan overlaid, and milestone icons appear in full color if impacted or greyed out if unchanged. Norman's "visibility of system status" and feedback principles demand the system continuously communicate its current state; a global selector that silently changes results across pages is a textbook violation.

**Implication for Ember.** Put a persistent scenario chip/selector in the global top bar, visible on every page whose numbers it affects. When a non-baseline scenario is active, change the visual treatment (e.g., a colored band or label) so the user can never mistake scenario output for baseline reality. Borrow ProjectionLab's comparison affordances: dashed baseline vs. overlaid scenario, and a line-by-line summary of what changed. Be explicit (per the Boglehead lesson) about which data is shared vs. scenario-specific.

### Principle 5 — A tightened button taxonomy (kill the 7×8 sprawl)

**Stated crisply:** Collapse to **three emphasis levels (primary, secondary, tertiary/ghost) plus two semantic variants (danger, and one inverse/contextual)**, across **three sizes (small for tables, medium default, large for marketing/hero)**. One primary action per view.

**Evidence.** Every major design system converges here. Carbon: "a layout should contain a single high-emphasis button"; use tertiary/ghost in "toolbars, data lists and dashboards" with many actions; danger buttons have dedicated styling; small size is "used in space constrained situations such as in tables." PatternFly: "limit primary buttons to one per page," danger buttons "for actions… potentially destructive or difficult/impossible to undo." The Vincit design-system case study shows precisely Ember's problem — uncontrolled variant×size permutations: "With five new style variants, that makes 5 × (4 + 5 + 5) = 70 potential new manifestations of our button" — and prescribes a tightened, documented taxonomy whose maintenance "is streamlined immensely by the use of design tokens." Hick's/cognitive-load logic: fewer, clearer choices reduce decision time and extraneous load.

**Implication for Ember.** Replace 7 variants × 8 sizes with: **emphasis** {primary, secondary, tertiary/ghost}; **semantic** {danger, inverse}; **size** {sm, md, lg}. Rule: exactly one primary per view; destructive actions use danger styling, separated by whitespace from constructive actions; in dense tables use ghost/tertiary icon buttons; overflow extra actions into a kebab menu (never hide the primary action). Encode the whole taxonomy in design tokens so it stays consistent.

### Principle 6 — Redundant semantic encoding; never color alone (finance color & tokens)

**Stated crisply:** Green/red gain–loss and all status colors must always be reinforced by a non-color cue (sign, arrow/icon, position, or label), and color must be delivered through semantic tokens that work in dark mode.

**Evidence.** WCAG 1.4.1 (Level A): color must not be "the only visual means of conveying information." Prevalence (Colour Blind Awareness): "approximately 1 in 12 men (8%) and 1 in 200 women." A practitioner audit cited a financial app whose red/green validation indicators were "indistinguishable" for users with tritanopia or in bright sunlight because "there was no text, icon, or other non-color indicator." Cleveland–McGill ranks color saturation last for quantitative judgment, reinforcing that color should be categorical/redundant. Dark-mode-first (Ember's choice) raises the stakes: saturated reds/greens on dark backgrounds need careful luminance contrast (WCAG 1.4.11 requires 3:1 for non-text UI elements).

**Implication for Ember.** Define semantic tokens (`--color-gain`, `--color-loss`, `--color-warning`, etc.) with dark-mode values tuned for contrast, and _always_ render gain/loss with a leading sign or triangle (▲/▼) and correct numeric sign — color is the reinforcement, not the message. This also satisfies the "tidy up design tokens" goal: a small, semantic, mode-aware token set replaces ad-hoc color usage.

### Principle 7 — Progressive disclosure everywhere dense data lives

**Stated crisply:** Default to summarized overviews; reveal granular detail (tax lots, transaction splits, assumption history, year-by-year rows) on demand.

**Evidence.** Shneiderman's visual-information-seeking mantra ("overview first, zoom and filter, then details-on-demand") is the canonical framework and is widely applied to dashboards. CLT supports it: progressive disclosure manages intrinsic load by sequencing. The mobile-table research is explicit — show "3–4 fields" to identify a row, then expose the rest "on a dedicated detail page, in a popup, or via an accordion." ProjectionLab applies this with drill-down tax analytics and per-account assumption overrides hidden until needed.

**Implication for Ember.** Holdings tables show position-level summary; expanding a row reveals tax lots. Activity shows transactions; expanding reveals splits. The money-flow waterfall shows the overview; clicking a node reveals the breakdown. Assumption fields show the current value and source badge; expanding reveals the append-only history. This is the mechanism that lets Ember be "dense but legible" without overwhelming.

### Principle 8 — Standardized empty, loading, and error states + consistent CRUD and primary-action placement

**Stated crisply:** Every list/table/page uses the _same_ templates for empty, loading, and error states; CRUD interactions (create/edit/delete + confirmation) follow one pattern; the primary "add" action sits in a predictable, consistent location.

**Evidence.** Norman's affordances/signifiers and Nielsen's consistency heuristic: users should not "have to wonder whether different words, situations, or actions mean the same thing." Error prevention heuristic: "present users with a confirmation option before they commit" to destructive/irreversible actions, with the safe option as default. Baymard's form research: use **live inline validation** but avoid premature validation (don't error a field "before they have had a chance to type a valid input"), and remove the error message the moment the input becomes valid; always use visible labels (never inline/placeholder-only labels) because of "loss of context." Recognition-over-recall (Nielsen) and information scent (Pirolli) argue the "add" button must be where users expect it.

**Implication for Ember.** Ship reusable `EmptyState`, `LoadingState` (skeletons that match the table's column structure to preserve layout and reduce perceived latency), and `ErrorState` components. Standardize CRUD: a consistent create/edit pattern (modal or side-panel — pick one), inline validation on blur (not on keypress, and cleared instantly on fix), and a delete confirmation for destructive actions with the consequence named in the button label and the safe option as default. Place the primary "add" action consistently (e.g., top-right of each section's content area) so it is discoverable by recognition, not search. Add breadcrumbs to close the documented wayfinding gap.

### Mobile density (cross-cutting)

For dense financial tables on mobile, the research prescribes a layered strategy: **column priority** (hide non-essential columns at breakpoints, keeping 2–3 key fields), **horizontal scroll with a frozen first column** for genuinely wide tables ("the header row stays fixed, so the user always knows which column they are reading"), and **card transforms** (each row becomes a labeled card) for sparse data. These should be configured _once_ in the unified table component (Principle 1), not re-solved per page. For Sankey/waterfall diagrams, which do not shrink gracefully, default to a simplified or vertically-stacked representation on small screens with tap-to-expand nodes (details on demand), rather than forcing a pinch-zoom of the full diagram.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Ratify the laws and fix the highest-trust-leverage items (now).**

1. Adopt the eight principles as written design-system law, documented with do/don't examples.
2. Resolve toast vs. banner with the two-channel rule (Principle 2) and delete the redundant path.
3. Promote **Assumptions** to a first-class destination separated from Settings/Profile (Principle 3), with prominent date-stamps/source-badges/history. _Benchmark to change course:_ if usability testing shows users still hunt for where to change a return rate (>1 in 5 fail a find-the-assumption task), elevate Assumptions to a top-level nav item rather than a Planning sub-area.

**Stage 2 — Unify components (next).** 4. Build the single `<DataTable>` with three density modes, tabular numerals, details-on-demand, and built-in column-priority/mobile behavior (Principles 1, 7, 8). 5. Collapse buttons to the 3+2 / three-size taxonomy in tokens (Principle 5). 6. Ship standardized empty/loading/error components and one CRUD pattern with inline validation + named-consequence delete confirmation (Principle 8).

**Stage 3 — Wayfinding and scenarios (next).** 7. Add a persistent global scenario indicator in the top chrome with distinct visual treatment for non-baseline scenarios, plus breadcrumbs (Principle 4). _Benchmark:_ if any user in testing mistakes scenario output for baseline reality, the indicator is insufficient — increase its prominence (color band, not just a label). 8. Define semantic, dark-mode-tuned color tokens; enforce sign/icon redundancy on all gain/loss and status displays (Principle 6).

**Keep-or-change the 7-section nav.** Keep Accounts, Holdings, Activity, Flows, Budget, Planning, Settings _as the investment-first split_ — it correctly front-loads the ledger and is consistent with how Kubera/ProjectionLab users think. But (a) surface Assumptions as its own anchor, and (b) consider whether "Flows" and "Budget" read as distinct to users (information-scent test) — if testing shows overlap confusion, merge or relabel. _Benchmark to revisit the whole IA:_ if first-click testing on the top 5 tasks (add account, edit a holding's tax lot, change a return assumption, compare two scenarios, find this month's cash flow) drops below ~80% correct first clicks, restructure.

## Caveats

- **Competitive-UX claims rest partly on review sites and the vendors' own help/changelog docs**, not first-hand audits; reviews can carry affiliate bias (several ProjectionLab and Kubera reviews are monetized). Where possible I relied on the products' own documentation (ProjectionLab help/changelog, Boldin release notes/help center) for behavioral specifics, and treated marketing adjectives skeptically.
- **One Boldin "intuitive interface" review conflicts with the broader "overwhelming/steep learning curve" consensus** (RetireBeforeDad, SmartAsset, MarriageKidsAndMoney); I weighted the consensus and the primary Bogleheads confusion thread over the single positive outlier.
- **Cleveland–McGill's hierarchy is a "framework within which to work," not a rigid prescription** — the authors themselves cautioned against treating the ranking as absolute; task, audience, and context modulate encoding effectiveness.
- **The academic principles are robust and durable**, but their _specific_ application to Ember (exact toast timing, exact breakpoints, modal-vs-panel for CRUD) should be validated with usability testing on Ember's real data, not assumed.
- These are **principles, not page designs** by intent; the per-page implementation (and exact thresholds above) should be set by the team and the downstream coding agent, then tested.
